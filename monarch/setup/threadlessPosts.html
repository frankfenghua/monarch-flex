Notes and asides: So I think I may be fortean
not in the sense that I am obsessed with the anomalous.

But in the way that I find myself very intrigued with the effects of the age of logic. The period from the mid 18th century to the dawn of the 20th.

I have come to the conclusion that the search for truth and the development of science was not only a new age for knowledge but also a destructive period for the unknown, the long held myths and traditions. The mysteries.

To live in a time where that which is now known as impossible was not yet limited. Where the imagination and tales of old were as worthy to be labeled as truth.

And the zeal at which the new found scientific priority slashed and burned the accumulated wisdom. Not in search of truth but of provability and replication under controlled conditions. Eliminating the potential for phenomena to exist as part of a whole and not in isolation.

For example when science attacked the idea of meteorites.
In the late 1700s there was such enthusiasm for debunking the idea of stones falling from the heavens that one would be hard pressed to find a specimen predating this period. They were hidden, shunned and disposed of.
this idea is clear in a quote from Antoine Lavoisier, the father of modern chemistry, when he told his fellow Academicians, "Stones cannot fall from the sky, because there are no stones in the sky!"

And those who claimed otherwise were considered holdbacks from a darker age of mysticism and fear.

Today I ask myself, at what cost do we know.
